“I can calculate the orbit of heavenly bodies, but not fathom the madness of men”

Sir Isaac Newton  [after the South Sea Company bubble]

 

 

 

A Declaration:

Going forward, I will write a new blog post every week. I think it’s important for me to set aside 30 minutes for self-reflection. I can jam out to some iTunes, reflect back on my week, and take a few precious moments to plan for the next week. There is no excuse.  It’ll give me a way—possibly, the only way—to understand my growth in dialogic terms. The open framework of blogging also provides the added benefit of accountability. If I were to voice my thoughts only to myself, it would be significantly more difficult to identify moments of cognative dissonance, i.e., on a metacognative level  I understand that I have a systematic bias that prejudices my understanding. And this is probably dangerous, right? I think, on a larger, this is the very thing  that, in Proctor’s agnatology, accounts for ignorance: we, because limitless information is now readily available, use information to construct “knowledge” around a scaffold of personal bias. I mean, this prejudice is even present in something as ostensibly objective as scientific research.

I think the first paper that I ever had to read for Dr. Roux was Platt’s paper on strong inference. It’s interesting that even scientists feel the need to develop ways for systematically rooting out conformation bias.  If anything, as you approach the margins of human knowledge, the value of consensus becomes increasingly irrelevant. It’s often at this fringe where we find individuals ready to challenge our orientation of the stars; it’s where we find the Galileos, the Einsteins, the Newtons, the painters of the universe. But I’m digressing—you’ll forgive my discrusiveness, I hope?

 

The Week in Reverse:

I’ve been swamped. It’s the first week back, so my counterparts and I have been trying to plan lessons for the next semester, trying to consolidate my projects, and deal with the looming vagaries surrounding the new bossman— In the coming weeks, because fate, kismet, whatever you would like to call the ever-weaving Moirai, hate me, I will also be tasked with entertaining my new mystery boss. In Thailand this most likely means lots of meetings and dinners. It also, probably, means dinner parties where Mr. Walker and his Thai pals Blend 285 and, unbeknownst to himself, the curiously named Hong Thong will be guests of honor. But before you misunderstand and cough entitled! let me explain. This whole charade will  most likely require a level of Thai that would elude me even if I could spend the next 100 years practicing. Beyond that, have I mentioned that we’re not really supposed to drink?

 

Thin Strings:

It is blistering hot in Thailand. And I have 2 thin, fragile wires keeping me from certain peril. But as long as my fan is spinning, I know that the combination of sweat—lovely imagery, I know—and scorching air being blown over my back will somehow eke out a win in favor of livable conditions. At least the sisters Fate seem to be enjoying their little dabble with irony: haha, thin wires are keeping my life in the balance, haha! At least if the fan goes out, I can take a deep breath and soak in the bucolic milieu of rural Asia—you know, with only small hints of burning trash and PAHs floating through my lungs. Keep me in your thoughts; this week, as you might be able to tell from my histrionic tone,  has been a bit rough.

 

On a Completely Unrelated Note About Organization . . .  . 

I told myself that I would start using RTM (remember the milk) to organize my life. It’s been somewhat successful. My migration from the usual Google tasks isn’t quite complete, but I’m finding that the added plugins are highly beneficial, e.g., using YAR, I’m currently tracking the amount of time that I’m spending on all of my activities; this lets me see a breakdown of my day in pie graph form.  And if you’re one of those GTD (Getting Things Done) fans, RTM is completely compatible. There are also some pretty sweet plugins that allow you to directly incorporate RTM into your Google apps, e.g., calendar and Gmail, too.

 

So yea, Tess, you might’ve been right all along: maybe more structure is helpful?

 

 

I’ll leave you with a picture of an adorable bird that flew into my room. The birds just been hanging out. Neat! I should probably get to work. . . .
